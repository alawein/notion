### Empirical Risk Minimization 

The theoretical foundation of supervised ML. We minimize the average loss over training data as a proxy for expected risk over the true distribution. 
R̂(f) = (1/n) Σᵢ L(f(xᵢ), yᵢ) + λΩ(f) - **λΩ(f): **regularizer — penalizes complexity to control generalization. 
- **Generalization gap: **R(f) − R̂(f). Controlled by model capacity and data size. 
Definition 
### Bias-Variance Tradeoff 
E[MSE] = Bias² + Variance + Noise - **Bias: **error from wrong assumptions. Fix: more complex model. 
- **Variance: **error from overfitting. Fix: regularization, more data. 
- **Diagnosis: **Learning curves — plot train vs. val loss as training data grows. Gap = variance. Both high = bias. 
02 Statistics & Probability Critical Foundation Statistics is the single most underestimated topic in DS interviews at the senior level. Interviewers at mature companies routinely fail candidates on probability and hypothesis testing because candidates have leaned too hard on ML frameworks and forgotten the foundations. STAT Explain p-value in plain English. What does it actually mean? BASIC ▶ Core Answer The p-value is the probability of observing results at least as extreme as yours *assuming the null hypothesis is true *. It is NOT the probability that the null hypothesis is true, nor the probability your result is due to chance. - **Correct interpretation: **"If the treatment had no effect, we'd see this result by random chance only 3% of the time." 
- **Common misinterpretation: **"There's a 3% chance our result is wrong." — This is false. 
- **Threshold α: **If p < α, reject H₀. α = 0.05 is convention, not law. Choose α based on cost of Type I error. 
- **p-value does not tell you: **effect size, practical significance, or probability of replication. 
Senior DS Expectation Be able to articulate the distinction between **statistical significance **(p < 0.05) and **practical significance **(effect size is large enough to matter for the business). A 0.1% conversion lift may be highly significant at n=10M but commercially irrelevant. STAT What is the Central Limit Theorem and why does it matter in practice? BASIC ▶ Core Answer The CLT states that the sampling distribution of the sample mean approaches a Normal distribution as sample size n → ∞, regardless of the underlying population distribution. This holds as long as the population has finite mean and variance. - **Why it matters: **Justifies using z-tests and t-tests even when the underlying data isn't normal. The mean of any large enough sample is approximately normal. 
- **Rule of thumb: **n ≥ 30 is often sufficient for symmetric distributions. Skewed distributions may need n ≥ 100+. 
- **Practical implication: **In A/B testing, CLT justifies treating the test statistic (difference in means) as normally distributed — the foundation of most frequentist testing frameworks. 
- **Failure mode: **Heavy-tailed metrics (revenue per user) may require larger n or non-parametric tests due to outliers. 
STAT Explain Type I vs. Type II errors and the power of a test. MID ▶ Core Answer - **Type I error (α): **False positive — reject H₀ when it's true. Controlled directly by your significance threshold. Cost: act on a non-effect. 
- **Type II error (β): **False negative — fail to reject H₀ when it's false. Cost: miss a real effect. 
- **Power = 1 − β: **Probability of detecting an effect that is truly there. Target ≥ 80% (β ≤ 0.20). 
- **Power increases with: **larger sample size, larger true effect size, lower variance in the metric, higher α threshold. 
- **Sample size calculation: **n ≈ (z_α/2 + z_β)² · 2σ² / δ² where δ = minimum detectable effect. 
Practical Framing "In a clinical trial, Type I = approving an ineffective drug. Type II = rejecting an effective one. In A/B testing, Type I = shipping a useless feature (wastes eng resources), Type II = missing a winning feature (opportunity cost). The business context determines which error is more costly." STAT What is the difference between MLE and MAP estimation? When do you use each? MID ▶ Core Answer MLE: θ̂ = argmax P(data | θ) → maximize likelihood of observed data MAP: θ̂ = argmax P(data | θ) · P(θ) → MLE + prior on parameters - **MLE: **No assumptions about θ. Pure data-driven. Equivalent to minimizing negative log-likelihood. 
- **MAP: **Incorporates prior knowledge. With Gaussian prior P(θ) ∝ exp(-λ‖θ‖²) → equivalent to L2 regularization. 
- **L1 regularization: **Equivalent to MAP with Laplace prior on θ — induces sparsity. 
- **When to use MLE: **Large datasets where data dominates. No strong prior beliefs. 
- **When to use MAP: **Small datasets. Domain knowledge about parameter ranges. Regularization needed.

CART splits on Gini impurity or entropy. TECH How does gradient boosting work? How is it different from bagging? MID ▶ Core Answer **Bagging **(Bootstrap Aggregating): Train many independent models on bootstrapped subsets, average their predictions. Reduces variance without changing bias. Random Forest = bagging of decorrelated decision trees (feature subsampling). **Gradient Boosting **: Train models sequentially, each fitting the residual errors of the previous ensemble. Each step adds a weak learner that corrects remaining mistakes. Reduces both bias and variance but more prone to overfitting. - **Key equation: **F_m(x) = F_{m-1}(x) + η·h_m(x) where h_m fits the negative gradient of the loss. 
- **Learning rate η: **Smaller = more trees needed, but better generalization. Typical: 0.01–0.1. 
- **When boosting beats bagging: **When bias is the dominant error (underfitting). Boosting explicitly reduces bias. 
- **When bagging beats boosting: **Noisy data, large datasets. Boosting amplifies noise; bagging is robust. 
TECH Explain regularization — L1, L2, Elastic Net, Dropout. Intuition and when to use each. MID ▶ Core Answer L1 (Lasso): λ Σ|wᵢ| → sparse weights (some exactly zero) → feature selection L2 (Ridge): λ Σwᵢ² → small uniform weights → stability, no sparsity Elastic Net: λ₁Σ|wᵢ| + λ₂Σwᵢ² → both: sparse + stable - **Why L1 → zeros: **The L1 ball has corners aligned with axes. The loss contour touches a corner → a weight becomes exactly zero. L2 ball is smooth — no corners → weights shrink but stay non-zero. 
- **Dropout: **During training, randomly zero out activations with probability p. Equivalent to averaging exponentially many sub-networks. Acts as L2 regularization on weights in linear networks. 
- **Weight decay: **L2 on parameters in the optimizer (AdamW) — preferred over adding L2 to loss for adaptive optimizers (decouples from gradient scale). 
- **Decision rule: **Believe few features matter → L1. All features contribute → L2. Uncertain → Elastic Net. Neural network → Dropout + Weight Decay. 
TECH How do you handle class imbalance? MID ▶ Core Answer — In Order of Try-This-First - **1. Change your metric first: **Accuracy on imbalanced data is useless. Use F1 (per class), AUC-PR, or explicit FPR/FNR targets before touching the data or model. 
- **2. Class weights in loss function: **`class_weight='balanced' `in sklearn, or manual `pos_weight `in PyTorch. Zero additional data needed. Should be your first intervention. 
- **3. Focal Loss: **Down-weights well-classified easy examples, focuses training on hard minority class examples. γ=2 standard. Better than class weights for extreme imbalance. 
- **4. Oversampling (SMOTE): **Synthesize minority class samples by interpolating between existing ones. Risk: synthetic samples can hurt calibration and create decision boundary artifacts. Validate carefully. 
- **5. Undersampling majority: **Only when majority class is genuinely redundant and you have ample data. Wastes information otherwise. 
- **6. Threshold tuning: **Move classification threshold below 0.5. Simple, effective, often overlooked. Plot precision-recall curve and choose operating point. 
Anti-Pattern Never evaluate on a test set that was oversampled. Always evaluate on real held-out data that reflects production distribution. Oversampling belongs only in training. 04 Deep Learning Neural Networks TECH Explain backpropagation and the vanishing gradient problem. MID ▶ Core Answer Backpropagation applies the chain rule of calculus to compute gradients of the loss with respect to every parameter: ∂L/∂wᵢ = ∂L/∂aₗ · ∂aₗ/∂aₗ₋₁ · … · ∂a₁/∂wᵢ. This product of Jacobians is computed from output to input (reverse mode autodiff). - **Vanishing gradients: **When gradient magnitudes are <1, repeated multiplication makes them exponentially small. Deep networks → early layers get near-zero gradient → learn nothing. 
- **Sigmoid/tanh cause this: **Saturated activations have derivative ≈ 0. ReLU fixes it: derivative is exactly 1 for positive inputs, 0 otherwise (no saturation region for positive x). 
- **Residual connections: **Skip connections provide a direct gradient path: ∂L/∂xᵢ = ∂L/∂xᵢ₊₁ + ∂L/∂xᵢ₊₁ · ∂F/∂xᵢ. The "+1" term prevents vanishing. 
- **Batch Normalization: **Normalizes activations within a batch → prevents saturation → stabilizes gradient magnitude. Also acts as implicit regularization. 
- **Gradient clipping: **Hard solution — clip gradient norm to a maximum value before update. Standard in RNNs and Transformers where gradient explosion can occur. 
TECH How does the Transformer attention mechanism work? Why does it scale quadratically? MID ▶ Core Answer Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √dₖ) · V - **Q, K, V: **Linear projections of input. Q = "what am I looking for," K = "what do I contain," V = "what I'll contribute." The dot product QKᵀ computes all pairwise compatibility scores. 
- **Quadratic scaling: **For L tokens, QKᵀ is an L×L matrix. Computing and storing it is O(L²·d). At L=1000 this is manageable; at L=100K (long documents) it becomes a bottleneck. 
- **√dₖ scaling: **Prevents dot products from growing large in high-d space, which would push softmax into near-zero gradient regions. 
- **Multi-head: **h independent attention heads with projections of size dₖ = d/h. Each head specializes in different relationships. Output = concat + linear projection. 
- **Solutions to O(L²): **Flash Attention (IO-aware tiling, same result faster), Longformer (sparse attention), Mamba (state-space model, linear in L). 
TECH Compare optimizers: SGD, Adam, AdamW. When does each win? MID ▶ Core Answer - **SGD + Momentum: **Simple gradient descent with momentum term. No adaptive LR. Requires careful LR tuning but often achieves best final test accuracy for vision tasks. Best with OneCycleLR schedule. 
- **Adam: **Adaptive learning rates per parameter using first moment (mean) and second moment (variance) of gradients. Converges faster with less tuning. Weakness: L2 regularization interacts incorrectly with adaptive scaling — weight decay doesn't work as intended. 
- **AdamW: **Adam with decoupled weight decay — applies weight decay directly to weights, not to gradients. Fixes Adam's L2 issue. Default for transformers. Generally preferred over Adam for any architecture using weight decay. 
- **Rule of thumb: **Training a transformer or LLM → AdamW. Training a CNN from scratch → SGD + Momentum (often better final accuracy). Quick experiments or fine-tuning → Adam. 
Key Insight "AdamW is not just Adam with different weight decay — it fundamentally changes the geometry of the optimization. With Adam, weight decay scales with the gradient magnitude. With AdamW, it doesn't. This matters especially for layers with small gradients." TECH What is overfitting and how do you detect and prevent it? BASIC ▶ Core Answer Overfitting: the model learns the training data too well, including noise, and fails to generalize. Training loss ↓ but validation loss ↑ or plateaus. - **Detection: **Plot train vs. validation loss over epochs. Divergence = overfitting. Measure on a held-out test set — if test performance significantly trails validation, your validation set may have been contaminated. 
- **Prevention (in order of try-first): **More data (best solution), early stopping, dropout, L1/L2 regularization, data augmentation, simpler model architecture, cross-validation. 
- **Data augmentation: **Expand training set by transforming existing samples (flip, rotate, crop for images; token masking for text). Increases effective dataset size for free. 
- **Early stopping: **Monitor validation loss; stop training when it stops improving. Restore weights from best validation checkpoint. Simple and always recommended. 
05 NLP & Large Language Models Your Differentiator Your Turing experience (SFT/RLHF pipelines, reward modeling, evaluation harnesses) is a genuine differentiator for a Senior DS role in 2026. Most DS candidates know scikit-learn and PyTorch. Far fewer have built LLM training pipelines from scratch. TECH What is the difference between BERT and GPT architecturally? What are they each good for? MID ▶ Core Answer - **BERT (encoder-only): **Bidirectional — each token attends to all other tokens. Pre-trained with Masked Language Modeling (predict masked tokens). Best for: classification, NER, question answering, embeddings. Can't generate text autoregressively. 
- **GPT (decoder-only): **Causal/unidirectional — each token attends only to past tokens. Pre-trained with next-token prediction. Best for: text generation, few-shot learning, instruction following. Used in all modern LLMs (GPT-4, Llama, Gemini). 
- **T5/Flan-T5 (encoder-decoder): **Encoder processes input bidirectionally; decoder generates output causally. Best for: translation, summarization, structured prediction (input → output transformation tasks). 
- **Why decoder-only won: **Simpler architecture scales better. Emergent few-shot capabilities at large scale. RLHF aligns well with autoregressive generation. 
TECH Explain the RLHF pipeline: SFT → Reward Model → PPO. What does each step accomplish? HARD ▶ Core Answer (Your Direct Experience) - **SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning): **Fine-tune the base pre-trained model on curated (prompt, ideal response) pairs. Teaches the model the desired output format, tone, and task format. Limitation: you can only teach what you can demonstrate. Hard cases in the demonstration data coverage gaps. 
- **Reward Model: **Train a separate model on (prompt, chosen_response, rejected_response) triplets — where a human annotator said A is better than B. The reward model learns the latent quality criterion and can score any new response. This generalizes to unseen cases beyond what demonstrations cover. 
- **PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization): **Use the reward model as a reward signal. Fine-tune the SFT model to maximize expected reward via RL. KL penalty against SFT reference prevents the model from "reward hacking" (finding ways to score high without being actually good). 
- **DPO (Direct Preference Optimization): **Mathematically equivalent to RLHF but eliminates the explicit reward model. Trains directly on preference pairs. More stable, simpler pipeline. Preferred for smaller datasets and domain adaptation. This is what you built patterns for at Turing/Morphism. 
Resume Bridge "At Turing, I built the reward modeling framework — specifically the dataset curation pipeline, annotation quality scoring, and the evaluation harness measuring response quality across 12 benchmark tasks. The DPO implementation I built at Morphism extends that work to domain adaptation with far fewer labeled pairs." TECH What is RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)? When do you use it vs. fine-tuning? MID ▶ Core Answer RAG augments LLM generation with retrieved context: query → retrieve relevant documents from a vector store → include them in the prompt → generate answer conditioned on both the query and retrieved context. The model doesn't need to memorize knowledge; it retrieves it at query time. - **Use RAG when: **Knowledge changes frequently (no retraining needed). Domain knowledge is large and can't fit in fine-tuning data. Need provenance/citations. Knowledge is proprietary (stays in your vector store, not in the model weights). 
- **Use fine-tuning when: **Changing model behavior, style, or format — not just knowledge. Internalizing specialized vocabulary. Task-specific output structure. RAG can't change how the model reasons, only what it knows. 
- **Hybrid: **Fine-tune for behavior + RAG for knowledge. Most production LLM systems use both. 
- **Vector store: **Embeddings of documents stored in Pinecone, Chroma, Qdrant, pgvector. Retrieval = approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search by cosine similarity. 
06 Experimentation & A/B Testing Senior DS Core A/B testing is one of the most common Senior DS interview topics because it requires combining statistics, product thinking, and engineering awareness. Companies run thousands of experiments per year — and most candidates don't understand them deeply enough to avoid common pitfalls. STAT Walk me through how you design and analyze an A/B test from scratch. MID ▶ Core Answer - **Step 1 — Define the metric: **Primary metric (what you're optimizing), guardrail metrics (what you can't let regress). Be specific: "7-day retention" not "engagement." 
- **Step 2 — Power analysis: **Compute required sample size given: baseline conversion rate, minimum detectable effect (MDE), α=0.05, power=0.80. n ≈ 16σ²/δ² for a two-sample z-test. 
- **Step 3 — Randomization: **Random assignment to control/treatment at user level (not session). Ensure randomization is independent of outcome — no leakage. 
- **Step 4 — Run for the pre-specified duration: **Do not stop early. Multiple testing increases Type I error (optional stopping problem). Use sequential testing (e-values) if you need early stopping capability. 
- **Step 5 — Check for contamination: **Network effects (users interact), novelty effects (early spike), survivorship bias (different attrition between groups). 
- **Step 6 — Analyze and report: **Point estimate + confidence interval. Effect size + practical significance. Segment analysis (does the effect differ by user cohort?). 
STAT What is p-hacking? How do you avoid it in an experimentation platform? HARD ▶ Core Answer P-hacking (or data dredging): performing many statistical tests or looking at results multiple times until finding p < 0.05, then reporting only that finding. At α=0.05, you expect 1 in 20 true null tests to appear significant by chance. Running 20 tests guarantees a false positive in expectation. - **Multiple testing correction: **Bonferroni: α_adjusted = α/k for k tests. Benjamini-Hochberg: controls false discovery rate (less conservative). Use when running many sub-group analyses. 
- **Pre-registration: **Document your hypothesis, primary metric, and sample size before seeing any data. Removes degrees of freedom. 
- **Optional stopping problem: **Peeking at results mid-experiment inflates Type I error. If p=0.048 after 3 weeks, stopping early and claiming significance is p-hacking. Use sequential/anytime-valid tests (e-values, SPRT) if you need continuous monitoring. 
- **Engineering guard: **Lock the experiment duration in your platform. Alert when sample size is reached. Require statistical sign-off before business decisions.

BEHAVIORAL How do you decide whether a problem is worth solving with ML at all? MID ▶ Core Answer - **Does a simple rule-based system work already? **If 80% accuracy is achievable with 10 if-statements and ML gets you to 85%, the cost may not be worth it. Start with the simplest thing that works. 
- **Is the data available, labeled, and representative? **A model is only as good as its training distribution. If the data doesn't exist or can't be labeled reliably, ML is premature. 
- **What's the ROI? **Estimate: business impact of improvement × probability of success × developer cost × maintenance cost. Senior DS can estimate all four.

- **Can you measure success? **If you can't define a metric that stakeholders agree represents improvement, the project will never ship with confidence.

The same framework applies to any ML vs. non-ML decision." 08 ML in Production Systems TECH A model performs great in testing but fails in production. What do you do? MID ▶ Diagnostic Framework - **First: don't assume the model is wrong. **70% of "model failures" in production are data pipeline bugs: preprocessing mismatch between training and inference, wrong feature normalization, missing feature handling. 
- **Check train-serve skew: **Log raw model inputs in production. Compare their distribution against training data. If the distributions diverge significantly → covariate shift. Not a model bug. 
- **Check the labeling: **If you collected labels after deployment, is your labeling process accurate? Ground truth drift is common and silent. 
- **Shadow mode: **Run old and new model in parallel. Log both outputs. Examine disagreements. The pattern of disagreement usually reveals the root cause within 50 cases. 
- **Rollback first, diagnose second: **If production is actively broken, roll back to last known-good version immediately. Diagnose from a stable state. 
- **Never retrain first: **Retraining before diagnosing root cause is random chance. You might fix it accidentally, or you might bake the bug into the new training data. 
TECH What is model drift and how do you monitor for it? MID ▶ Core Answer - **Data drift (covariate shift): **P(X) changes — the input distribution shifts. Model isn't wrong; it's seeing data it wasn't trained on. Detect with: PSI (Population Stability Index), KS test, or Jensen-Shannon divergence on feature distributions. 
- **Concept drift: **P(Y|X) changes — the relationship between features and target changes. Harder to detect without labels. Proxy: if model confidence drops systematically, concept drift may be occurring. 
- **PSI (Population Stability Index): **`PSI = Σ(Aᵢ - Eᵢ) · ln(Aᵢ/Eᵢ) `— PSI < 0.1: stable, 0.1–0.2: investigate, >0.2: retrain. 
- **Output monitoring: **Track distribution of model predictions. A shift in predicted class probabilities without a corresponding shift in inputs signals label drift or concept drift. 
- **Business metrics: **Model accuracy → business outcome (clicks, conversions). If the business metric diverges from accuracy → model may be fitting the wrong proxy. 
TECH Describe a CI/CD pipeline for ML. What tests do you run before deploying a model? HARD ▶ Core Answer - **Data validation: **Schema checks, distribution checks vs. baseline, missing value rates, label quality metrics. No data, no model — this is the first gate. 
- **Unit tests on pipeline: **Test preprocessing transforms on synthetic inputs. Test that pipeline handles edge cases (empty input, all-null feature, extreme values). 
- **Model performance gates: **Accuracy ≥ baseline, precision/recall above thresholds, no regression vs. current production model on held-out benchmark. These gates are non-negotiable. 
- **Behavioral tests (slicing): **Performance must hold across demographic slices, temporal slices, and known hard subgroups. Aggregate accuracy can hide failures on important subpopulations. 
- **Shadow deployment: **Run new model alongside production model for N days. Compare output distributions. Only promote if they agree on >95% of inputs and disagree on expected cases. 
- **Canary release: **Route 1–5% of traffic to new model. Monitor business and model metrics for 24–48h. Progressive rollout to 10% → 50% → 100% with monitoring gates. 
09 Data Engineering & Pipelines Practical TECH What makes a data pipeline production-ready? What do most DS pipelines get wrong? MID ▶ Core Answer - **Idempotency: **Re-running the pipeline on the same data produces the same result. No side effects from retries. This is what makes recovery from failures predictable. 
- **Provenance / lineage: **Every output knows exactly where it came from — which input data, which code version, which parameters. You need to be able to reproduce any output. This was my core lesson from 2,300+ DFT jobs at Berkeley. 
- **Schema validation at ingestion: **Reject malformed data before it enters the pipeline. Silent corruption propagates all the way to model training. Loud failures at ingestion are infinitely better than silent bias in the training set. 
- **Monitoring: **Track row counts, null rates, distribution statistics per run. Automated alert if today's pipeline run produces significantly different statistics from last week's. 
- **What most DS pipelines get wrong: **Train-serve skew (different preprocessing at inference vs. training), no schema contracts, no data lineage, secrets in code, no automated testing of the pipeline itself.

11 General Technical Interview Q&A Interview Prep All Technical Statistics Behavioral Resume TECH What is cross-validation and when does k-fold fail? MID ▶ Core Answer - **K-fold CV: **Split data into k equal folds. Train on k-1 folds, evaluate on the held-out fold. Repeat k times. Final score = average across folds. Gives a better estimate of generalization than a single train/val split. 
- **When k-fold fails — time series: **Random splits create leakage — future data predicts the past. Use TimeSeriesSplit (always train on past, validate on future). 
- **When k-fold fails — group data: **If the same user/entity appears in both train and test folds, the model memorizes entity-specific patterns. Use GroupKFold to keep all records from one entity in the same fold. 
- **When k-fold fails — class imbalance: **Random folds may have very few positive examples. Use StratifiedKFold to preserve class ratios in each fold. 
- **Nested CV: **Outer loop estimates generalization. Inner loop tunes hyperparameters. Prevents hyperparameter overfitting on the validation set. Computationally expensive but correct. 
TECH Explain the curse of dimensionality. How do you address it? MID ▶ Core Answer In high-dimensional space, data becomes increasingly sparse. The volume of a unit hypersphere relative to the bounding hypercube → 0 as dimensions increase. Almost all data points are far from each other and near the "edges" of the feature space. - **Consequence for KNN: **Distance becomes meaningless — every point is approximately equidistant from every other. KNN degrades rapidly above ~20 features. 
- **Consequence for density estimation: **Exponentially more data needed to maintain the same density. 100 points in 1D is dense; in 10D, you need 100¹⁰ for the same density. 
- **Solutions: **Feature selection (remove irrelevant features), PCA/UMAP (project to lower-dimensional manifold), regularization (penalizes relying on many weak features), kernel methods (work in infinite-dimensional space without explicitly computing coordinates). 
- **When deep learning helps: **DNNs implicitly learn a low-dimensional manifold representation of the data. The curse applies to the learned representation, which is much lower-dimensional than the raw input.

How are they learned and used? MID ▶ Core Answer An embedding is a dense, low-dimensional continuous representation of a discrete or high-dimensional object (word, user, product, document). Semantically similar items map to nearby vectors in embedding space. - **How learned: **As part of a downstream task (word2vec: predict context words; BERT: masked prediction). The embedding layer is just a lookup table that gets tuned end-to-end with the objective. 
- **Word2Vec key insight: **"king" − "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen". Arithmetic in embedding space captures semantic relationships because co-occurrence statistics encode meaning. 
- **Use in production: **User/item embeddings for recommendation (collaborative filtering via dot product). Document embeddings for semantic search (ANN lookup in vector store). Entity embeddings for tabular ML (encode categories better than one-hot). 
- **Transfer learning: **Pre-trained embeddings (from BERT, Llama, CLIP) are frozen or fine-tuned on downstream tasks. Starting from good embeddings dramatically reduces the data needed for a new task. 
TECH Explain feature engineering. What makes a good feature? MID ▶ Core Answer - **A good feature: **Predictive of the target. Available at inference time (no leakage). Stable — doesn't fluctuate wildly between train and deployment. Interpretable to domain experts. 
- **Common techniques: **Interaction features (A×B), polynomial features, log transforms for skewed data, binning continuous features, lag features for time series, rolling statistics (mean, std over window). 
- **Leakage (the worst bug): **Using information at training time that wouldn't be available at inference time. E.g., using "account balance after transaction" to predict if a transaction will be fraudulent. Performance looks great in training, fails in production. 
- **Deep learning and feature engineering: **CNNs, Transformers learn features automatically. For tabular data, good manual features still beat raw features in most cases, especially with XGBoost. 
- **Feature store: **Production system that computes, stores, and serves features consistently for both training and inference. Prevents train-serve skew. Companies like Uber (Michelangelo), Airbnb (Zipline) built these. 
TECH What is the difference between Bayesian and frequentist statistics? HARD ▶ Core Answer - **Frequentist: **Probability = long-run frequency of events. Parameters are fixed (unknown constants). Uncertainty is about the data, not the parameters. p-values and confidence intervals are frequentist constructs. 
- **Bayesian: **Probability = degree of belief. Parameters are random variables. We update our belief about parameters given data: P(θ|data) ∝ P(data|θ) · P(θ). The posterior P(θ|data) quantifies uncertainty about the parameter. 
- **Confidence interval vs. credible interval: **Frequentist CI: if we repeated this experiment many times, 95% of intervals would contain the true parameter. Bayesian CI: 95% probability that the parameter is in this range (given our prior and data). The Bayesian one is the intuitive interpretation people want. 
- **Practical preference: **Frequentist: easier to compute, no need to specify prior, standard in industry A/B testing. Bayesian: natural incorporation of prior knowledge, directly answers "what is the probability the variant is better?", better for small data or sequential decision-making.

14 Traps, Saves & Questions to Ask Tactics Universal Danger Zones Trap 1: Vague without numbers "We significantly improved performance" tells an interviewer nothing and signals lack of rigor. Every technical story needs a number: percentage improvement, time saved, samples processed, dollars. Trap 2: Memorized definitions without understanding If you can recite "p-value is the probability the null is true" but can't explain why that's wrong, you'll get caught. Know the concept well enough to be challenged. Trap 3: Only knowing the happy path Interviewers specifically ask about failures, tradeoffs, and limitations. "The model worked great" as a final answer is a red flag. Have a failure, a tradeoff, and a limitation ready for every major project. Trap 4: Proposing complex solutions before simple ones "I'd build a transformer-based model" as the first proposal signals you don't think in baselines. Always start with: rule-based, logistic regression, or a simple statistical test. Justify complexity only when baseline is insufficient. Trap 5: Negative about previous employers Zero exceptions. Even Turing. Even if justified. "The project scope changed" is the ceiling. Move on in under 30 seconds. Recovery Scripts — When You're Stuck Recovery Playbook 
### What to do when you don't know the answer 
- **"I haven't encountered that specific case, but reasoning from first principles…" **Then think out loud. Shows methodology, not recall. 
- **Bound the problem: **"The constraints are X. The goal is Y. I'd approach it by first establishing Z." Even a wrong answer that shows clear reasoning scores better than a memorized fact.
