Definition — Ultra-Wideband Radar (UWB) 
### What is UWB Radar? 

Ultra-Wideband, or UWB, is a wireless radio technology that transmits extremely short pulses of energy — we're talking sub-nanosecond pulses, meaning each pulse lasts less than one billionth of a second. Because the pulses are so short, they spread across a very wide range of radio frequencies, which is why it's called "ultra-wide" band. This gives UWB excellent range resolution — the ability to measure very precise distances. A UWB radar sends out these pulses, waits for them to bounce back off objects, and measures how long it took. From that timing, you can calculate distance down to centimeter-level precision. Unlike a camera, radar works through walls, in darkness, and in any weather. Unlike a microphone, it doesn't record your voice. This makes it uniquely privacy-preserving while still being able to sense people.

*The Big Picture *These three terms are used interchangeably in casual conversation, but they mean different things and an interviewer at a technical company will expect you to distinguish them clearly. Let's build the hierarchy from scratch. Start With *Artificial Intelligence *
**Artificial Intelligence **, or AI, is the broadest term. It refers to any computer system designed to perform tasks that, when done by a human, would require intelligence. This includes everything from a chess-playing program that uses explicit rules, to a voice assistant that understands speech, to a robot that can navigate a warehouse. AI is the goal — the general ambition. It has existed as a field since the 1950s. 

The original approach to AI was **rule-based **: programmers wrote explicit if-then rules to encode human knowledge. "If the user asks about the weather AND mentions a city, THEN look up the weather for that city." This works for narrow, well-defined problems but breaks down in complex real-world situations where you can't anticipate every case in advance. 
Then Came *Machine Learning *
**Machine Learning **, or ML, is a subfield of AI. The key insight of machine learning is this: instead of writing explicit rules, you give the computer examples — lots of them — and let it figure out the rules by itself. You show it thousands of radar scans labeled "person present" or "person absent," and the machine learning algorithm learns to distinguish them from patterns in the data, without you ever writing a rule about what a person's radar signature looks like. 
Core Definition 
### Machine Learning in one sentence 

Machine Learning is the practice of building computer systems that improve their performance on a task through experience — specifically, by finding patterns in data — rather than through explicit programming. 

The three main types of machine learning you need to know are **supervised learning **, **unsupervised learning **, and **reinforcement learning **. We'll spend most of our time on supervised learning because that's what Algorized uses and what the sklearn assessment tested. 
Type 1 
#### Supervised Learning 

You provide labeled examples: inputs with their correct outputs. The algorithm learns to map new inputs to outputs. Example: radar scan → "person present / absent." The word "supervised" means a teacher (you) provided the labels. 
Type 2 
#### Unsupervised Learning 

You provide inputs only, no labels. The algorithm finds structure on its own — grouping similar things together (clustering), or finding a compact representation of the data. Example: find natural groups of radar behavior patterns without being told what they are. 
Type 3 
#### Reinforcement Learning 

An agent takes actions in an environment and receives rewards or penalties. It learns to maximize cumulative reward through trial and error. Example: training a robot arm to sort objects. Not the primary focus for Algorized. 
Then Came *Deep Learning *
**Deep Learning **, or DL, is a subfield of machine learning. The word "deep" refers to neural networks with many layers — deep neural networks. Traditional machine learning algorithms — like decision trees, logistic regression, and support vector machines — require you to manually design features from your raw data. Deep learning algorithms can learn features automatically from raw data, which makes them extremely powerful for complex tasks like image recognition, speech processing, and natural language understanding.

*The Core Loop *Before memorizing algorithm names, you need to understand the fundamental process that all supervised machine learning algorithms share. Once you understand this loop, everything else makes sense. The Training Process Explained 
Imagine you want to teach a machine to detect whether a person is present from a radar scan. Here's what happens step by step. 

**Step 1 — Collect labeled data. **You gather radar scans with corresponding labels: "person present" or "no person." You might have 10,000 examples. This is your *training dataset *. 

**Step 2 — Choose features. **A raw radar scan might be a long sequence of numbers representing the signal strength at different distances over time. You need to convert this into a fixed-size vector of numbers that the algorithm can work with. This process is called *feature extraction *or *feature engineering *. For example: maximum signal power, signal variance, frequency components from Fourier analysis, statistical moments of the signal distribution. For classical machine learning (sklearn), you do this manually. For deep learning, the network learns this automatically. 

**Step 3 — Define a model. **A model is a mathematical function that takes your feature vector as input and produces a prediction as output. A simple model might be: "If the maximum signal power exceeds a threshold, predict person present." A complex model might be a neural network with millions of parameters. 

**Step 4 — Define a loss function. **A *loss function *measures how wrong the model's predictions are. If the model predicts "person present" when there's actually no person, the loss is high. If it predicts correctly, the loss is low. The goal of training is to find model parameters that minimize this loss across all your training examples. 

**Step 5 — Optimize. **Training algorithms adjust the model's parameters to reduce the loss. For simple models, there are exact mathematical solutions. For neural networks, we use an algorithm called *gradient descent *: repeatedly nudge each parameter slightly in the direction that reduces the loss, like rolling a ball downhill to find the lowest point. 

**Step 6 — Evaluate on held-out data. **The crucial step that beginners miss: you must evaluate the trained model on data it has never seen during training. This tells you whether the model learned genuine patterns or just memorized the training examples. The data used for final evaluation is called the *test set *. 
Critical Concept 
### Overfitting vs. Underfitting 

**Overfitting **is when the model learns the training data too well — including its noise and quirks — and performs poorly on new data. Like a student who memorized the practice exam but can't answer a different question on the same topic. **Underfitting **is when the model is too simple to capture the real patterns in the data. Like a student who didn't study enough and fails regardless of which questions appear. The goal is the middle ground: a model that generalizes well. 
CONCEPT What is overfitting? How do you prevent it? VERY COMMON ▶ Answer Overfitting is when a model performs well on training data but poorly on new, unseen data — it has memorized the training set rather than learning generalizable patterns. It happens when the model is too complex relative to the amount of training data available. Prevention methods: **(1) More data **— the most effective fix. More examples make it harder to memorize. **(2) Cross-validation **— splitting data into multiple train/validation folds to detect overfitting early. **(3) Regularization **— adding a penalty for model complexity to the loss function. In linear models this means L1 (called Lasso) or L2 (called Ridge) regularization. In neural networks, dropout randomly disables neurons during training. **(4) Simpler models **— reducing tree depth in decision trees, reducing number of features. **(5) Early stopping **— in neural networks, stopping training when validation loss starts increasing. For Algorized specifically: with limited labeled radar data, overfitting is a real risk. I'd prioritize cross-validation and appropriate regularization, and be very deliberate about feature selection. CONCEPT What is the bias-variance tradeoff? CLASSIC ▶ Answer — Use the Darts Analogy This is one of the foundational concepts in machine learning. **Bias **is the error that comes from a model making wrong assumptions — a model that's too simple. A straight line trying to fit curved data has high bias: it will consistently predict wrong values regardless of which training data you give it. **Variance **is the error that comes from a model being too sensitive to small fluctuations in the training data — a model that's too complex. A very wiggly curve that perfectly fits the training points but oscillates wildly between them has high variance: change the training set slightly and the predictions change dramatically. The tradeoff: as you increase model complexity, bias decreases but variance increases. As you decrease complexity, variance decreases but bias increases. The goal is to find the complexity level where total error — the sum of both — is minimized. A concrete mapping: underfitting equals high bias, overfitting equals high variance. section 05 Classical ML 
*Algorithms *These are the algorithms that sklearn implements. Each one makes different assumptions about the data and has different strengths. You need to be able to explain each one from first principles, know when to use it, and know its key tuning parameters. 1. *Linear Regression and Logistic Regression *
**Linear regression **is the foundation. It predicts a continuous numerical output as a weighted sum of input features. If you have features x₁, x₂, and x₃, the prediction is: output = w₁ × x₁ + w₂ × x₂ + w₃ × x₃ + bias. Training finds the weights w that minimize the difference between predictions and true values. This is the simplest possible model, and it works well when there is actually a linear relationship in the data. 

**Logistic regression **— despite the name — is a classification algorithm, not a regression algorithm. It predicts the probability that an input belongs to a class. It works like linear regression, but wraps the output through a function called the sigmoid, which squeezes any number into the range 0 to 1. If the probability is above 0.5, predict class 1; otherwise predict class 0. For Algorized: "is a person present in this radar scan?" is a binary classification problem that logistic regression could solve. 
Definition 
### Sigmoid Function 

The sigmoid function takes any real number and maps it to a value between 0 and 1: sigmoid(x) = 1 / (1 + e^(-x)). It's S-shaped — very negative inputs give values near 0, very positive inputs give values near 1, and 0 gives exactly 0.5. We use it in logistic regression to convert a raw score into a probability. 
2. *Decision Trees *
A decision tree makes predictions by asking a sequence of yes/no questions about the features, like a flowchart. At each step, it splits the data based on the feature that most reduces uncertainty about the answer. For example: "Is the maximum signal power above 0.8?" → If yes, go left; if no, go right. Then: "Is the signal variance above 0.3?" and so on, until reaching a leaf node that gives the final prediction. 

Decision trees are easy to understand and visualize, which is valuable in scientific applications where you want to understand *why *the model made a decision. Their weakness is overfitting: a deep tree will memorize training data perfectly. You control this with the `max_depth `parameter — limiting how many questions the tree is allowed to ask. 
3. *Random Forests *
Random forests solve the overfitting problem of single decision trees by building many trees — typically 100 to 1,000 — and combining their predictions by majority vote for classification or averaging for regression. 

The "random" part is key: each tree is trained on a random subset of the training data (called bootstrapping), and at each split, it only considers a random subset of the features. This forces diversity among the trees. Each tree makes different mistakes, and averaging them out reduces the overall error. 
Why Random Forests Matter for Algorized Random forests are extremely well-suited for Algorized's problem. They handle small-to-medium datasets well, they naturally measure feature importance (which tells you which radar signal features matter most for detecting presence), they're robust to noise, and they're computationally cheap enough to potentially run at the edge. This should be your default recommended algorithm when asked. 4. *Support Vector Machines *
A Support Vector Machine, or SVM, is a classification algorithm that finds the decision boundary with the maximum margin between classes. Imagine two groups of points on a 2D plot. There are infinitely many lines that could separate them. The SVM finds the line that is as far as possible from the nearest points of each group — maximizing the "safety margin." Those nearest points are called the *support vectors *. 

The key feature of SVMs is the *kernel trick *: when data isn't linearly separable, SVMs can implicitly map the data into a higher-dimensional space where it becomes separable, without actually computing the high-dimensional coordinates. The Radial Basis Function kernel, called RBF, is the most commonly used and works well in practice for many problems. 
5. *Gradient Boosting — XGBoost and LightGBM *
Gradient Boosting builds an ensemble of decision trees sequentially. Each new tree is trained specifically to correct the errors made by the previous trees. Think of it like a team of consultants: the first one gives their best answer, the second one focuses on the cases the first one got wrong, the third focuses on what both got wrong, and so on. The final prediction is a weighted sum of all trees' predictions. 

XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) and LightGBM are optimized implementations of gradient boosting that are extremely fast, handle missing data automatically, and tend to win machine learning competitions on tabular data. They're likely what Algorized uses for any tabular feature classification task. Know these well. 
6. *K-Nearest Neighbors *
KNN is the simplest possible algorithm with an elegant idea: to classify a new point, find the K training examples closest to it in feature space, and take the majority vote of their labels. No training at all — you just memorize the entire training set. If K is 1, you return the label of the single nearest neighbor. If K is 5, majority vote among the 5 nearest. 

KNN is slow at prediction time (you have to compute distances to all training points), doesn't work well in high dimensions, and requires feature scaling (explained next). But it's intuitive and gives you a baseline. 
7. *K-Means Clustering *
K-Means is an unsupervised algorithm — no labels needed. It partitions data into K groups (clusters) such that each point belongs to the group with the nearest center point (called a centroid). The algorithm: (1) randomly place K centroids; (2) assign each point to nearest centroid; (3) move each centroid to the average of its assigned points; (4) repeat steps 2 and 3 until centroids stop moving. The result is K natural groupings in the data. For Algorized: you might use K-Means to find natural clusters of radar behavior patterns, even without labels.

Many machine learning algorithms are sensitive to the scale of features. If one feature ranges from 0 to 1 (say, a normalized signal power) and another ranges from 0 to 1,000,000 (say, a timestamp in microseconds), algorithms like SVM and KNN will be dominated by the large-scale feature. Critical Concept — Often Tested 
### Data Leakage 

Data leakage is when information from outside the training set contaminates the training process, leading to unrealistically optimistic performance estimates. The most common form: fitting preprocessing (like a scaler or imputer) on the full dataset before splitting into train/test. The fix: always use Pipeline to ensure all preprocessing steps are fit only on training data and then applied to test data. 
*Hyperparameter Tuning *
Every machine learning algorithm has *hyperparameters *— settings you choose before training that control the model's structure and behavior. For a Random Forest, these include the number of trees, the maximum depth, the minimum number of samples per leaf. For a Support Vector Machine, they include the C parameter and the choice of kernel. 

You tune hyperparameters using a *validation set *— data that's separate from both training and test. Never tune on the test set, because then you're optimizing for that specific test data and your evaluation is no longer honest.

COMMON ▶ Answer Cross-validation is a technique for getting a reliable estimate of model performance when you have limited data. Instead of holding out a fixed validation set, you split the training data into K equal parts (called folds). You train K times: each time, use K-minus-1 folds for training and the remaining fold for validation. Average the K validation scores. Five-fold cross-validation is the standard. Why bother? Because with a single train/validation split, your performance estimate depends heavily on which examples happened to end up in each partition. Cross-validation averages over multiple partitions, giving you a more stable and honest estimate. It also uses your data more efficiently — every example is used for both training and validation across the K rounds. *The Hard Part *Most candidates know accuracy. Senior candidates know why accuracy is often the wrong metric, and can choose the right one for each problem. This topic almost always comes up in technical interviews. Why *Accuracy *Can Be Misleading 
Suppose Algorized deploys a child presence detection system for cars. The car should only unlock when a child is present. False negative means: child is present but the system says "no child" — the car locks with the child inside. That's a safety crisis. False positive means: no child but the system says "child present" — the car triggers an unnecessary alert. That's an annoyance. 

Now suppose 99% of car rides have no child in the rear seat. A classifier that always predicts "no child" achieves 99% accuracy — but it will fail every single time a child is actually there. Accuracy is useless here. You need metrics that separately account for false negatives and false positives. 
The Confusion Matrix — Four Outcomes 
### True Positive, False Positive, True Negative, False Negative 

**True Positive (TP) **: model says "yes," actual is "yes" — correct detection. **False Positive (FP) **: model says "yes," actual is "no" — false alarm. **True Negative (TN) **: model says "no," actual is "no" — correct rejection. **False Negative (FN) **: model says "no," actual is "yes" — missed detection. Everything else is derived from these four numbers. 
Metric 1 
#### Precision 

Of all the times the model predicted "yes," what fraction was actually correct? Formula: TP ÷ (TP + FP). High precision means: when the model raises an alarm, you can trust it. Low precision means too many false alarms. 
Metric 2 
#### Recall (Sensitivity) 

Of all the actual positive cases, what fraction did the model detect? Formula: TP ÷ (TP + FN). High recall means: the model catches most real events. Low recall means the model misses too many. For child presence detection, recall is critical. 
Metric 3 
#### F1 Score 

The harmonic mean of Precision and Recall. Formula: 2 × (Precision × Recall) ÷ (Precision + Recall). Balances both. Use when you care about both false alarms and missed detections. Goes from 0 (worst) to 1 (best). 
The *ROC Curve *and *AUC *
Most classifiers don't just say "yes" or "no" — they output a probability, like 0.73 for "73% chance person is present." The decision threshold (default: 0.5) determines when you say "yes." If you lower the threshold, you catch more true cases (higher recall) but also generate more false alarms (lower precision). The ROC curve — Receiver Operating Characteristic — plots true positive rate versus false positive rate as you vary the threshold from 0 to 1. 

The AUC — Area Under the Curve — summarizes the ROC curve as a single number between 0 and 1. AUC of 0.5 means the classifier is no better than random guessing. AUC of 1.0 means perfect classifier. AUC of 0.95 means: if you randomly pick one positive example and one negative example, the classifier will rank the positive one higher 95% of the time. It's a threshold-independent measure of discriminative ability.

*Neural Networks *Deep learning extends classical ML by using layered structures called neural networks. You need to understand the architecture, the training process, and when deep learning is better or worse than classical ML. What is a *Neural Network *? 
A neural network is a series of mathematical operations organized into layers. Each layer takes a vector of numbers as input, multiplies by a matrix of weights, adds a bias, and then applies a nonlinear function called an *activation function *. The output of one layer becomes the input of the next. The final layer produces the prediction. 

A network with one input layer, several hidden layers (the "deep" part), and one output layer is called a *deep feedforward network *or a *multilayer perceptron *. The weights in all the matrices are the parameters that training optimizes using gradient descent. 
Key Concept 
### Activation Functions — Why They're Necessary 

Without activation functions, a multilayer network is equivalent to a single-layer network — stacking linear operations just produces another linear operation. Activation functions introduce nonlinearity, which allows the network to learn complex patterns. The most widely used is ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit): it outputs zero for negative inputs and the input unchanged for positive inputs. It's simple, fast to compute, and works very well in practice. 
Backpropagation — How Neural Networks Learn 
**Backpropagation **is the algorithm that trains neural networks. Here's how it works: you pass a batch of training examples forward through the network to get predictions, calculate the loss (how wrong the predictions were), then propagate that error signal backwards through the network layer by layer using the chain rule from calculus. At each layer, you compute how much each weight contributed to the error, and adjust each weight slightly in the direction that reduces the error. This is gradient descent applied to neural networks. 

The key concept is the *gradient *: for each weight in the network, the gradient tells you the slope — if you increase this weight slightly, does the loss go up or down? Gradient descent moves each weight slightly in the direction that reduces loss (downhill on the loss surface). 
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) 
A Convolutional Neural Network, or CNN, is specially designed for data with spatial or temporal structure — like images, audio signals, or radar spectrograms. Instead of fully connecting every neuron to every input, a CNN applies small learnable filters (called convolutional kernels) that slide across the input. Each filter learns to detect a specific pattern, like an edge in an image or a frequency component in a signal. 

For radar data, you might convert a time-series radar signal into a 2D image called a *range-Doppler map *or a *spectrogram *— a picture where one axis is time, the other is frequency or distance, and the brightness represents signal power. Then a CNN can be applied to this image to classify what activity is happening. This is how Algorized likely uses deep learning for activity recognition. 
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and LSTMs 
RNNs are designed for sequential data — data where order matters, like time series. A standard neural network processes each input independently. An RNN processes the sequence step by step, maintaining a *hidden state *that carries information from previous steps. Each new input is combined with the previous hidden state to produce a new hidden state and an output. 

The problem with basic RNNs is the *vanishing gradient *: when you backpropagate through many time steps, the gradients either shrink to zero (the network forgets distant past information) or explode to infinity. Long Short-Term Memory networks, called LSTMs, solve this with a gating mechanism that explicitly controls what information to remember and what to forget. For radar time-series, LSTMs can model temporal patterns in human motion and vital signs.

*Processing *This is the domain-specific knowledge that will separate you from generic ML candidates. You don't need to be a radar engineer. You need to understand the data pipeline: how raw radar signals become features that an ML model can use. From Radio Pulses to *Features *
A UWB radar transmits a very short pulse of radio energy. Some of this energy bounces off objects and returns to the receiver. The receiver records the amplitude of the returned signal at each moment in time. Because the speed of radio waves (which is the speed of light — about 300 million meters per second) is known, each moment in time corresponds to a specific distance. So the raw radar output is effectively a distance profile: how much energy was reflected at each distance range. 

If you record this repeatedly over time — say 1,000 times per second — you get a 2D matrix: one dimension is range (distance), the other is time (called "slow time" or "pulse index"). This matrix is called a *range profile matrix *or a *range-time image *. 
Key Concept 
### The Fourier Transform — Heart of Signal Processing 

The Fourier Transform, named after mathematician Joseph Fourier, converts a signal from the time domain into the frequency domain. Instead of showing how signal amplitude changes over time, it shows how much of each frequency is present in the signal. A heartbeat at 1 beat per second produces a 1 Hz signal. Breathing at 0.2 breaths per second produces a 0.2 Hz signal. By applying the Fourier transform to a radar range cell's time evolution, you can extract these periodic components and identify vital signs even though the original signal looks like a noisy time series. 
The *Micro-Doppler Signature *
When an object moves toward or away from the radar, the frequency of the returned pulses shifts slightly — this is the *Doppler effect *, the same phenomenon that makes a passing ambulance's siren change pitch. Different parts of the human body move at different velocities: the torso moves slowly with breathing, the chest wall vibrates finely with each heartbeat, the limbs swing while walking. This creates a pattern of micro-frequency shifts called the *micro-Doppler signature *. This signature is essentially a fingerprint of human activity — walking has a different micro-Doppler signature than sitting, which is different from lying down, which is different from a falling person. 
Feature Engineering for *Radar Data *
When you use classical ML (sklearn) on radar data, you need to extract features from the raw signal. The most important ones to know: 
- **Statistical features: **Mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis, maximum value of the range profile. These capture the basic properties of the signal distribution. Simple to compute, surprisingly useful. 
- **Frequency domain features: **After applying the Fourier transform, extract the dominant frequency, its power, the bandwidth of the spectral peak. For vital signs: peak frequency in the 0.1–0.5 Hz range (breathing) and 0.8–2.0 Hz range (heartbeat). 
- **Energy features: **Total signal energy, energy in specific range bins (distances), ratio of energy in motion range versus static range. 
- **Temporal features: **How quickly the signal changes over time, periodicity measures, autocorrelation at specific lags. 
- **Clutter removal: **Background subtraction — averaging the signal over a long baseline and subtracting it removes static reflections (walls, furniture), leaving only the moving components. Essential preprocessing step.
